02 — THE P.O.N.T.E. EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

does AI add unique value?

A step-by-step model for deciding whether AI belongs in the surface at all. The honest answer is sometimes no — and the rubric is most useful in the moments when the team wants the answer to be yes.

P
Patterns

"Is there a user need and an identifiable pattern of behavior?"

What are the common issues users encounter? Can these be traced to identifiable patterns? Is there data supporting them?

O
Outcome

"What is the definition of success?"

What metrics will indicate success? What OKRs will this impact? How is user satisfaction measured?

N
Need

"Can a non-AI solution work better?"

What were the limitations with traditional systems? Does AI offer something unique? Or does the solution feel over-engineered?

T
Type

"Automation (speed) or augmentation (creativity)?"

Automation replaces human tasks. Augmentation assists humans with tasks they already do, making them better at their jobs.

E
Equity

"How do we ensure fairness and reduce bias?"

A tour-suggestion AI should not keep recommending one specific tour and never others. Bias in input becomes bias in output, at scale.

03 — CASE STUDY · PRICE PREDICTIONS

the AI predicts. the human decides.

How Google Flights uses HCAI to augment, not replace, human decision-making — and why it's the pattern most travel surfaces should be copying.

EXAMPLE · GOOGLE FLIGHTS DECISION PARALYSIS

The problem

Travelers repeatedly re-check prices, unsure if they're getting the best deal. The anxiety of "what if it gets cheaper?" creates hesitation loops — refreshing the same query, abandoning the cart, returning two days later.

The HCAI solution

A small price-confidence card that surfaces a prediction and gets out of the way:

PRICE INSIGHT
Lower than usual.
Prices are currently low for your dates.

The AI provides a prediction. The human retains full control of the decision.

Key takeaway

The AI predicts; the human decides. Augmentation over automation. The same pattern is portable to any high-anxiety commerce moment — insurance add-ons, travel-protection upsells, return windows.

04 — PRINCIPLES

six guardrails.

Ethical guardrails for responsible AI implementation. These are not obstacles to design — they're the conditions under which design earns user trust over time.

Transparency

Explain why the AI made the suggestion. Clear reasoning for decisions and actions.

Accountability

Clear lines of responsibility for AI errors. Organizations stay accountable for impact.

Privacy

Data used ethically and with consent. Strict adherence to privacy standards.

Human Autonomy

Empower users by enhancing abilities. Provide control over AI-assisted processes.

Beneficence

Aim to benefit individuals and society. Actively improve well-being; avoid harm.

Accessibility

Intuitive, user-friendly interfaces that cater to varying abilities and backgrounds.

"Our goal is not to have or use AI but to ensure we all understand it as a tool for solving our problems."
