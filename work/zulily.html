Zulily was the personalization-engine job in disguise as an email-marketing job. The brand sent ~16 million emails a day across the US, UK, AU, NZ, and DE. None of them were authored by a human. Every send — subject line, hero block, three lower stories, CTA, even the order of brand logos in the footer — was assembled per-recipient from a deterministic pipeline that read from inventory, segments, and a per-user click-history vector.

I was a designer on a team of ~9 across product, engineering, and data science. The work was finding the seams in the rendering pipeline where human judgement still mattered — copy templates, tile layouts, the 28 subject-line strategies — and making those primitives expressive enough that the algorithm had a wide range of well-formed sends to choose from. The mockup above is the operator's view: 42 segment pools × 28 subjects × 96 hero variants × 4 lower stories per recipient, scored, ranked, and sent.

the work

  • The hero-block kit. A composable visual system for the top of every email: 12 layout primitives × 4 type pairings × 6 background treatments. The combinatorics gave the engine a wide selection space without any send looking off-brand.
  • The 28 subject-line strategies. Authored as templates with named variables ({seg.label}, {wishlisted_brand}, {cart_item.title}). The engine picked the strategy whose embedding was closest to the recipient's recent click vector.
  • Tier signals. Lapsed 30/60/90, new mom, home category, wishlisted brand back-in-stock, abandoned cart at $30 / $50 / $100 thresholds. Each tier surfaced a different visual primitive for the same product feed.
  • The QA dashboard. The view above. Operators ran a simulated send at 09:00 PT every morning, eyeballed 16 random recipients per segment cohort, flagged any send that read off, and the engine retrained against the flag.

the result

+14% CTR, +45 seconds median session, and a steady drop in the operator headcount needed to ship a daily send to zero. The team that used to spend three hours a morning drafting hero blocks moved to designing the next set of primitives.

The brief was "make the email more personal." The actual work was making the personalization pipeline more composable, then trusting it.

archived

Zulily wound down its US operation in 2023. The personalization engine survives in the codebase the buyer acquired; the work above is the version that shipped between 2021 and 2022 against a 16M/day load.

WHAT I OWNED
01

THE SEGMENTATION MODEL

Designed how customers were segmented for personalized email targeting. Worked with the ML team on the model itself, owned the design surface that surfaced the segmentation logic to merchandising and marketing teams.

02

SEND-TIME OPTIMIZATION

Designed the system that determined when each customer received each email. Real-time signals, customer rhythm detection, send-time experimentation framework.

03

CONTENT SELECTION LOGIC

Designed how products were selected for each customer's email. Hundreds of thousands of items in catalog, dozens of slots per email, real-time relevance computation.

OUTCOMES
  • +14% CTR uplift across the email ecosystem
  • +45 seconds average session length post-click
  • zero manual curation required for personalized emails
  • 16M sends/day at production scale
WHAT THIS TAUGHT ME

ML personalization at this scale is a systems-design problem disguised as a content problem. The interesting design work is upstream of what the customer sees — in the rules, the rhythms, and the rubrics that determine relevance. The pixels follow from the system, not the other way around.
